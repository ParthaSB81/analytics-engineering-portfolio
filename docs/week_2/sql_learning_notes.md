## When to Use CTEs
Through this project, I learned to use CTEs when they make complex SQL queries easier to write, read, and maintain.

1. Break complex logic into smaller steps
Instead of writing one large query, divide it into logical stages.

2. Reuse intermediate results
When the output of one query is needed in the next step.

Example:

Monthly Revenue
        ↓
Running Revenue
        ↓
Revenue Growth %

3. Improve readability
Instead of deeply nested subqueries, give each step a meaningful name.

Example:

customer_orders
↓
repeat_customers
↓
repeat_purchase_rate

This makes the query much easier to understand.

4. Use window functions on aggregated data

5. Perform multi-stage calculations
Some business metrics require several calculations.

Examples:

Customer Lifetime Value (LTV)
Repeat Purchase Rate
Monthly Revenue Growth
Pareto (80/20) Analysis
Consecutive Revenue Decline
Top N Products per Category


## How I Calculate Business Metrics

When calculating any business metric, I follow a structured approach to ensure the metric is both technically correct and aligned with business requirements.

1. Understand the Business Question

I first identify what the business wants to measure and why the metric is important.

Example:

Revenue by State
Repeat Purchase Rate
Customer Lifetime Value
Monthly Active Customers

A clear business definition prevents incorrect assumptions before writing SQL.

2. Define the Metric

I write down the exact business definition of the metric.

Example:

Customer Lifetime Value (LTV) = Total revenue generated by a customer across all completed orders.

This ensures consistency in reporting.

3. Determine the Grain

I identify the level at which the metric should be calculated.

Examples:

Metric	Grain
Monthly Revenue	Month
Revenue by State	State
Seller Revenue	Seller
Customer Lifetime Value	Customer
Product Revenue	Product

Choosing the correct grain is one of the most important steps in analytics.

4. Choose the Correct Fact Table

The fact table should represent the business event being measured.

Business Metric	Fact Table
Revenue	fact_orders
Product Performance	fact_order_items
Payment Analysis	fact_payments
5. Select the Correct Measure

I choose the business measure that accurately represents the metric.

Examples:

Revenue → SUM(total_order_value)
Product Revenue → SUM(price)
Payment Contribution → SUM(payment_value)
Active Customers → COUNT(DISTINCT customer_unique_id)
6. Identify the Required Dimensions

Dimensions determine how the metric will be analyzed.

Examples include:

Date
Customer
Product
Seller
State
Payment Method
7. Apply Business Rules

Business metrics often require additional filtering or rules.

Examples:

Include only delivered orders.
Exclude undefined payment methods.
Ignore negative delivery delays.
Count customers using customer_unique_id instead of customer_id.
8. Implement the SQL Logic

Once the business definition is finalized, I choose the appropriate SQL techniques:

GROUP BY for aggregations.
Window functions for rankings, running totals, and period comparisons.
CTEs to break complex calculations into logical steps.
CASE expressions for classification.
Joins to combine fact and dimension tables.
9. Validate the Results

Finally, I validate the output by:

Comparing totals with source data.
Checking row counts.
Testing edge cases.
Verifying the results against expected business behavior.
